# Tile Theory at Two-Shanten (2)

Next comes the pattern with **one completed set and four taatsu blocks**.

When there are no obvious floating tiles, the choice is basically between two plans:

<strong class="red">(1) Drop the weakest taatsu.</strong>  
<strong class="blue">(2) Break a three-tile taatsu block back into a single taatsu and keep all four taatsu blocks.</strong>

Recent research tends to favor line **(1)**, so this site also treats **(1)** as the default approach.

Now let us look at actual hands.

If you think in terms of “drop the weakest taatsu,” the pinzu block is clearly worth keeping, because together with the shanpon it has 8 tiles of acceptance.

So the comparison is between the manzu and souzu kanchan blocks.  
The souzu side changes into a ryanmen more easily.

On the manzu side, even drawing <img src="../../hai/man5.gif" width="24" height="34" /> only creates another nido-uke shape, so the correct play is to drop the manzu block by cutting <img src="../../hai/man2.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

If you understand the rule “drop the weakest taatsu,” you should not hesitate here either.

You compare pair blocks in the same way: by the kinds of good-shape improvements they allow.

For example, the pair of <img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34" /> can become a three-sided wait on a draw of <img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34" />, and
<img src="../../hai/pin1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin7.gif" width="24" height="34" />
are all still effective tiles.

By contrast, the isolated pair
<img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" />
has only two direct acceptance tiles, making it the weakest taatsu block.

So the correct answer is to cut <img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

And even if you later draw <img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34" />, cutting <img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /> is still the best line.

Still, there are cases in real play where comparing taatsu is not easy.

Either cut can be justified.

So in this case, cutting <img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34" /> and postponing the decision is also a strong option.

In fact, simulation shows that directly dropping a kanchan is slightly better for raw tenpai speed.

However, if later on either <img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" /> or <img src="../../hai/pin8.gif" width="24" height="34" /> becomes thin, you can still adjust according to the live situation.

Also, if the hand later ends up waiting on

So cutting <img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34" /> here is not bad at all.

### Theory

<div class="green" markdown="1">
For a two-shanten hand with one completed set, one completed head, and four taatsu blocks,  
the basic rule when there are no floating tiles is to drop the weakest taatsu.  
However, if the taatsu comparison itself is difficult, in other words if there is no clearly weak taatsu,  
you can also fix a ryanmen and postpone the decision.
</div>
