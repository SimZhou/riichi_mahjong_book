# Taatsu Theory (2)

This page is about **ryan-kan** shapes.

A ryan-kan is a composite shape made from two kanchan blocks, such as <img src="../../hai/man2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man6.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

Its acceptance count is 8 tiles, but because:

(1) it uses three tiles  
(2) if it remains as the final wait, it becomes a kanchan wait

for those two reasons, in functional terms it is still nowhere near as good as a ryanmen shape.

## 1. Ryan-kan and Shanpon

**Example 1**  
<img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin7.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou6.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34" /> Tsumo <img src="../../hai/sou5.gif" width="24" height="34" />

The real question with a ryan-kan shape is often whether to take it as a ryan-kan or as a shanpon-style acceptance.

If we only look at the chance of reaching Pinfu, Example 1 seems to suggest cutting <img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

Drawing <img src="../../hai/man2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man4.gif" width="24" height="34" /> gives you a Pinfu tenpai.  
By contrast, after cutting <img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" /> or <img src="../../hai/man5.gif" width="24" height="34" />, only one tile type leads to a Pinfu tenpai.

So at first glance, taking the ryan-kan seems better.

But if you judge by tenpai efficiency, the correct play here is to cut <img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

That is because drawing <img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin4.gif" width="24" height="34" /> turns the hand into a complete one-shanten shape for Pinfu.

<img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin7.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou6.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34" /> Tsumo <img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34" />

When the pair is a middle tile that can still turn into a ryanmen,  
it is generally more favorable to take the shape as **kanchan + shanpon** rather than as a ryan-kan, because that gives you more chances to improve into a good shape.

In an open hand, shanpon-style acceptance becomes even more favorable.

In Example 3, you cut <img src="../../hai/sou5.gif" width="24" height="34" /> and aim for a pon-ten on <img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /> and <img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

### Theory Summary

<div class="green" markdown="1">
When choosing between shanpon-style acceptance and kanchan-style acceptance in a ryan-kan hand,  
compare the number of good-shape improvements.  
In general, shanpon is favorable more often.
</div>

## 2. Easy-to-Miss Ryan-kan Shapes
