# Early-Hand Tile Theory (1)

One of the hardest parts of tile theory in hands that are three-shanten or worse is deciding how much weight to give to yaku value.  
Even if your current acceptance becomes a little narrower, the speed to tenpai can reverse once calling is taken into account.

To keep the conclusions as clear as possible, this page discusses the problem from the standpoint of **menzen efficiency**.

As an introduction to early-hand tile theory, let us start with hands that still contain several floating tiles.

---

When a hand still has isolated tiles, you can simply compare the functions of the remaining isolated tiles.

Here,
<img src="../../hai/man7.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/nan.gif" width="24" height="34" />
are the isolated tiles that do not currently belong to a set, taatsu, or pair.

The weakest one is obviously <img src="../../hai/nan.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

<img src="../../hai/nan.gif" width="24" height="34" /> can only improve by drawing another <img src="../../hai/nan.gif" width="24" height="34" /> to make a pair or triplet.

The suited tiles are clearly more valuable because they can become sequences.  
So the only correct discard here is <img src="../../hai/nan.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

Cutting <img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /> would not create some huge loss immediately, but if you draw
<img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou3.gif" width="24" height="34" />
without drawing <img src="../../hai/sou4.gif" width="24" height="34" />, you would fail to make one set.  
There is simply no reason to cut <img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /> here.

**Example 2**  
<img src="../../hai/man2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man6.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pei.gif" width="24" height="34" /> Tsumo <img src="../../hai/man4.gif" width="24" height="34" /> => Cut <img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" />

Cutting <img src="../../hai/pei.gif" width="24" height="34" /> would also be completely acceptable.

However, in this hand, whether you later draw <img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34" /> or <img src="../../hai/sou3.gif" width="24" height="34" />, the eventual discard will still be <img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

In other words, just like an honor tile, it has almost no use except pairing up.

If that is the case, then <img src="../../hai/pei.gif" width="24" height="34" /> may actually be slightly more valuable.  
If someone riichis on the next turn, you can immediately discard it. Even if it pairs up first, it can still become two safe tiles later.

### Theory Summary

<div class="green" markdown="1">
An isolated honor tile is the hardest kind of tile to turn into a set.  
If you have a wind tile that does not give you yaku, discarding it is normally absolutely correct.  
However, if there is a suited tile that is significantly more useless,  
you can still choose the suited tile first with future defense in mind.
</div>

**Example 3**  
<img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin9.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou6.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou6.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34" /> Tsumo <img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34" /> => Cut <img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" />

There are four isolated tile types here, so you cut the least valuable one.

For suited tiles, the general relationship is:

`1 and 9 < 2 and 8 < 3 to 7`

So <img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" /> and <img src="../../hai/pin9.gif" width="24" height="34" /> are the leading candidates.

Which of those two you cut first is not extremely important.  
But because there is already a suji tile in <img src="../../hai/man4.gif" width="24" height="34" />, even drawing both <img src="../../hai/man2.gif" width="24" height="34" /> and <img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /> will not create a real loss.

By contrast, if you cut <img src="../../hai/pin9.gif" width="24" height="34" />, then drawing both <img src="../../hai/pin7.gif" width="24" height="34" /> and <img src="../../hai/pin8.gif" width="24" height="34" /> would cost you one full set.

So the standard discard is to cut <img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" /> first.

**Example 4**  
<img src="../../hai/man6.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man9.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin6.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin9.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou9.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/haku.gif" width="24" height="34" /> Tsumo <img src="../../hai/pin1.gif" width="24" height="34" /> => Cut <img src="../../hai/pin1.gif" width="24" height="34" />

The isolated tiles here are
<img src="../../hai/pin1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin9.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou9.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/haku.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

If you look only at menzen riichi efficiency, even the yakuhai <img src="../../hai/haku.gif" width="24" height="34" /> could be discarded.  
But in a hand this weak, the speed boost from pairing a yakuhai and calling on it is too important to ignore.

So as long as there are other useless tiles left, you should not discard the yakuhai first.

You keep <img src="../../hai/sou9.gif" width="24" height="34" /> because there is no tile like
<img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34" />
that can properly support it. That leaves the pinzu tiles for comparison.

There are suji tiles on both sides in
<img src="../../hai/pin4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin6.gif" width="24" height="34" />,
but <img src="../../hai/pin6.gif" width="24" height="34" /> is already tied up inside the complete set
<img src="../../hai/pin4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin6.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

If you cut <img src="../../hai/pin9.gif" width="24" height="34" />, you lose out in the case where you draw
<img src="../../hai/pin7.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin8.gif" width="24" height="34" />
without drawing
<img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin6.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

That makes <img src="../../hai/pin1.gif" width="24" height="34" /> the least valuable tile.

**Example 5**  
<img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin6.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin7.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin9.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou6.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34" /> Tsumo <img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /> => Cut <img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34" />

When comparing suited tiles against each other, you should preserve the more connected shape.

Even if both are terminal tiles, a one-gap connected shape is still stronger than a completely isolated terminal tile in both acceptance and good-shape improvement.  
That point was already covered in [Chapter 1: Four-Tile Composite Shapes](../kihon/kihon10.md).

Also, in an ordinary sequence-based hand, an isolated terminal tile is always worse than any pair or taatsu.  
Of course, this does not apply when yaku such as Honitsu are involved.

Trying to build your hand around tiles like
<img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man5.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34" />
is generally a very disadvantageous choice.

### Theory Summary

<div class="green" markdown="1">
From the viewpoint of tile theory, an isolated terminal tile is worth less than any taatsu or pair.
</div>

<img src="../../hai/man2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin6.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou9.gif" width="24" height="34" /> Tsumo <img src="../../hai/man5.gif" width="24" height="34" /> => Cut <img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" />

In Example 6, the comparison is between
<img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" />
and
<img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

The key is the tile you just drew: <img src="../../hai/man5.gif" width="24" height="34" />.

If you cut <img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" />, then drawing <img src="../../hai/man7.gif" width="24" height="34" /> afterward still creates a kanchan taatsu.

But if you cut <img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34" />, then drawing <img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34" /> afterward becomes a complete loss.

That is another example of why holding isolated floating tiles along a suji line is disadvantageous.  
This was already discussed in [Chapter 2: The Theory of Floating Tiles](../pairi/pairi03.md).

The difference between cutting <img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34" /> and <img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34" /> is not huge, but cutting <img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34" /> is simply a bad move.

It is basic mahjong knowledge that a `7` tile is more useful than an `8` tile.  
And here there is also Iipeikou value involved, so the yaku loss is significant.

For some reason, some people still feel uncomfortable keeping the shape this way...

**Example 7**  
<img src="../../hai/man2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man7.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/pin8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou4.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34" /> Tsumo <img src="../../hai/sou6.gif" width="24" height="34" /> => Cut <img src="../../hai/man2.gif" width="24" height="34" />

What you need to watch out for in a shape that combines an ankou with a four-tile connected shape is the case where the hand already has two or more pairs.

In that case, you no longer need another head, so you cannot evaluate the connected shape as “one set plus one pair.”  
You must evaluate it as a block that still has to produce **two complete sets**.

Once you see it that way, it becomes clear that this block is actually quite hard to use.

<img src="../../hai/man3.gif" width="24" height="34" /> is thin enough already, and even if you draw
<img src="../../hai/man1.gif" width="24" height="34" /><img src="../../hai/man4.gif" width="24" height="34" />,
the resulting shape is still awkward.

By contrast, the isolated <img src="../../hai/pin8.gif" width="24" height="34" /> is better, because at least it still has the possibility of improving on a draw of <img src="../../hai/pin7.gif" width="24" height="34" />.
