# Pon and Chi (2)

## Which Pair Is Better to Save for Pon?

When you have multiple pairs that could potentially be used for pon,

it is usually better to keep the pair that is easier to pon.

Honor tiles are the easiest to pon,

and terminal tiles with low utility are also easier to expect from the table.

Sometimes the way you call even changes the final wait.

For Example 3, when <img src="../../hai/sou5.gif" width="24" height="34"> is discarded,

you might be tempted to choose between taking <img src="../../hai/ysou5.gif" width="32" height="24"><img src="../../hai/sou3.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou4.gif" width="24" height="34"> to make a <img src="../../hai/sou5.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34"> wait,

or taking <img src="../../hai/ysou5.gif" width="32" height="24"><img src="../../hai/sou6.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34"> to make a <img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou5.gif" width="24" height="34"> wait.

But in fact, both are wrong.

If you take it as a kanchan chi, the hand becomes a sanmenchan.

Especially in flush hands, it is common for a tile to be callable as a ryanmen chi and also as a kanchan or penchan chi.

Most of the time, the kanchan or penchan chi is better,

but sometimes the ryanmen chi is the correct choice.

In Example 4, if <img src="../../hai/man7.gif" width="24" height="34"> is discarded,

it is a big mistake to take it with <img src="../../hai/man6.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34"> as a kanchan chi.

Here, if you do not take it as a ryanmen chi, the <img src="../../hai/man9.gif" width="24" height="34"> wait disappears completely.

Shapes like this may not come up all the time,

but whenever you chi, get into the habit of checking:

**“Does the remaining shape change depending on how I take this chi?”**
