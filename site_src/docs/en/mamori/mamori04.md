# Suji (2)

This page goes one step deeper into suji.  
Suji is one of the most important concepts in mahjong, but it is also one of the easiest to misuse.

---

## Distant Suji?

This is a very common beginner misunderstanding.

For example, against the riichi below:

![1s](../../hai/sou1.gif)![White](../../hai/haku.gif)![West](../../hai/sha.gif)![8s](../../hai/sou8.gif)![6m](../../hai/man6.gif)![Red 6p](../../hai/ypin6.gif)

Since ![6m](../../hai/man6.gif) has already been discarded, ![3m](../../hai/man3.gif) and ![9m](../../hai/man9.gif) are suji.  
That means they are at least somewhat safer, because they will not deal into a ryanmen wait.

But even if ![8s](../../hai/sou8.gif) has been discarded, you still cannot say that ![2s](../../hai/sou2.gif) and ![5s](../../hai/sou5.gif) are safe.  
Sometimes you see people call this "distant suji" and cut those tiles, but it does not mean anything.

That said, even among non-suji tiles, there is still a danger difference between ![5p](../../hai/pin5.gif) and ![5s](../../hai/sou5.gif).

![5p](../../hai/pin5.gif) can hit:

- ![3p](../../hai/pin3.gif)![4p](../../hai/pin4.gif)
- ![6p](../../hai/pin6.gif)![7p](../../hai/pin7.gif)

both types of ryanmen.

By contrast, for ![5s](../../hai/sou5.gif), one side, the ![6s](../../hai/sou6.gif)![7s](../../hai/sou7.gif) ryanmen, has already been ruled out,  
so it is at least a little better.

For reference, here is the suji chart in table form:

| Discarded Tile | Suji |
| --- | --- |
| `4` | `1` and `7` |
| `5` | `2` and `8` |
| `6` | `3` and `9` |
| `1` and `7` | `4` |
| `2` and `8` | `5` |
| `3` and `9` | `6` |

## Safer Suji

**<Opponent's riichi discards>**

![1s](../../hai/sou1.gif)![White](../../hai/haku.gif)![West](../../hai/sha.gif)![8s](../../hai/sou8.gif)![6m](../../hai/man6.gif)![Red 5p](../../hai/ypin5.gif) Dora ![1p](../../hai/pin1.gif)

Let us use this riichi as the example.

### Example 1

![3m](../../hai/man3.gif)![3m](../../hai/man3.gif)![9m](../../hai/man9.gif)![9m](../../hai/man9.gif)![1p](../../hai/pin1.gif)![2p](../../hai/pin2.gif)![3p](../../hai/pin3.gif)![6p](../../hai/pin6.gif)![7p](../../hai/pin7.gif)![2s](../../hai/sou2.gif)![2s](../../hai/sou2.gif)![3s](../../hai/sou3.gif)![4s](../../hai/sou4.gif) Tsumo ![2s](../../hai/sou2.gif)

Since ![6m](../../hai/man6.gif) has already been discarded, both ![3m](../../hai/man3.gif) and ![9m](../../hai/man9.gif) are suji.  
So which one should you discard first?

- ![3m](../../hai/man3.gif) can hit: a ![3m](../../hai/man3.gif) tanki, a shanpon involving ![3m](../../hai/man3.gif), and also ![1m](../../hai/man1.gif)![2m](../../hai/man2.gif) or ![2m](../../hai/man2.gif)![4m](../../hai/man4.gif)
- ![9m](../../hai/man9.gif) can hit: a ![9m](../../hai/man9.gif) tanki, or a shanpon involving ![9m](../../hai/man9.gif)

Since ![9m](../../hai/man9.gif) no longer carries extra penchan or kanchan risk, it is clearly safer.  
So here, the correct discard is ![9m](../../hai/man9.gif).

Some people might wonder whether a ![3m](../../hai/man3.gif) tanki really happens.

![1m](../../hai/man1.gif)![1m](../../hai/man1.gif)![1m](../../hai/man1.gif)![3m](../../hai/man3.gif) ![2m](../../hai/man2.gif)![2m](../../hai/man2.gif)![2m](../../hai/man2.gif)![3m](../../hai/man3.gif) ![3m](../../hai/man3.gif)![4m](../../hai/man4.gif)![4m](../../hai/man4.gif)![4m](../../hai/man4.gif)

In composite shapes like this, it is entirely possible.

### Theory

**Terminal tiles are safer than other number tiles, because they have no kanchan or penchan risk.**

![1s](../../hai/sou1.gif)![White](../../hai/haku.gif)![West](../../hai/sha.gif)![8s](../../hai/sou8.gif)![6m](../../hai/man6.gif)![Red 5p](../../hai/ypin5.gif) Dora ![1p](../../hai/pin1.gif)

### Figure 2

![2m](../../hai/man2.gif)![3m](../../hai/man3.gif)![7m](../../hai/man7.gif)![8m](../../hai/man8.gif)![2p](../../hai/pin2.gif)![2p](../../hai/pin2.gif)![4p](../../hai/pin4.gif)![6p](../../hai/pin6.gif)![8p](../../hai/pin8.gif)![8p](../../hai/pin8.gif)![9p](../../hai/pin9.gif)![9p](../../hai/pin9.gif)![9p](../../hai/pin9.gif) Tsumo ![4m](../../hai/man4.gif)

Here, you are holding both ![2p](../../hai/pin2.gif) and ![8p](../../hai/pin8.gif), and both count as suji.

- ![2p](../../hai/pin2.gif) can hit: a ![2p](../../hai/pin2.gif) tanki, a shanpon involving ![2p](../../hai/pin2.gif), and ![1p](../../hai/pin1.gif)![3p](../../hai/pin3.gif)
- ![8p](../../hai/pin8.gif) can hit: a ![8p](../../hai/pin8.gif) tanki, a shanpon involving ![8p](../../hai/pin8.gif), and ![7p](../../hai/pin7.gif)![9p](../../hai/pin9.gif)

However, ![9p](../../hai/pin9.gif) is **already fully visible in all four copies, including the dora indicator**,  
so a kanchan ![8p](../../hai/pin8.gif) wait cannot exist.

On the other hand, because the dora is ![1p](../../hai/pin1.gif), a kanchan ![2p](../../hai/pin2.gif) wait looks very plausible.

If someone had a double-kanchan shape like ![1p](../../hai/pin1.gif)![3p](../../hai/pin3.gif)![5p](../../hai/pin5.gif),  
they would often cut ![5p](../../hai/pin5.gif) and riichi.

Shapes like the following are also entirely possible:

![1p](../../hai/pin1.gif)![1p](../../hai/pin1.gif)![1p](../../hai/pin1.gif)![3p](../../hai/pin3.gif) ![1p](../../hai/pin1.gif)![3p](../../hai/pin3.gif)![3p](../../hai/pin3.gif)![3p](../../hai/pin3.gif)

So ![2p](../../hai/pin2.gif) is not just a "suji, so it is safe" tile at all.  
It is actually a **very dangerous tile**.

From Figure 2, the correct discard is ![8p](../../hai/pin8.gif).

### Theory

**Tiles next to the dora are dangerous even when they are suji. Suji-trap riichi is also very common.**
