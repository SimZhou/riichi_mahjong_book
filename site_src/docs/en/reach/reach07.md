# Choosing Riichi Waits (1)

Over the next two pages, let us think about how to choose a wait when declaring riichi.

---

## Judgment 1: Number of Winning Tiles

When the number of winning tiles differs, choose the side with more winning tiles whenever possible.

If you cut <img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34">,
you can make it a wait on <img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34">.

But a 7-tile wait is better than a 4-tile wait,
so the correct answer is to cut <img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34"> and riichi.

Also, remember this souzu shape.
It comes in handy from time to time.

## Judgment 2: Point Value

When the number of winning tiles is the same, the higher-scoring option is better.

### Example 2

Cutting <img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34">, <img src="../../hai/pin4.gif" width="24" height="34">, or <img src="../../hai/pin6.gif" width="24" height="34">
all create the same 4-tile wait.

If that is the case, then riichi after cutting <img src="../../hai/pin4.gif" width="24" height="34">,
so that you still have the possibility of tsumo sanankou.

This pinzu shape is also worth memorizing.
It is very useful in practice.

There are real differences in how easy tiles are to discard, which means how easy they are to ron on.
The table above is a good rough guide.

Strictly speaking, even the tiles from 3 to 7 have small differences,
but they are minor enough that you do not need to worry about them much.

Tiles that are harder to use efficiently tend to get discarded more often.
In other words, <span class="b">yaochu tiles are easier to ron on.</span>

So in Example 3, compared with waiting on the hard-to-discard E-rank tiles <img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/pin6.gif" width="24" height="34">,

it is easier to win by waiting on the easier-to-discard <img src="../../hai/pin1.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34">.

That means the correct riichi is to cut <img src="../../hai/pin5.gif" width="24" height="34">.

How easy a tile is to come out is extremely important.
If you understand it, it creates edges in many situations.

In Example 4, if you look only at efficiency,
it may seem better to cut <img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34"> and allow a future tsumo <img src="../../hai/man5.gif" width="24" height="34"> to make a ryanmen.

But purely in terms of how easy it is to ron,
it is absolutely easier to take the shanpon on <img src="../../hai/sou9.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/man8.gif" width="24" height="34">.

This is an important piece of knowledge,
and you should be able to use it in real play.

### Example 5

<img src="../../hai/man4.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/man5.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/pin2.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/pin5.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/pin6.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/pin7.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou1.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou2.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou3.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou9.gif" width="24" height="34"> Draw <img src="../../hai/man6.gif" width="24" height="34"> Dora <img src="../../hai/pin7.gif" width="24" height="34">

Between a kanchan wait and a shanpon wait,
the shanpon is generally superior.

In Example 5, unless the table situation strongly suggests otherwise,
riichi after cutting <img src="../../hai/sou9.gif" width="24" height="34"> will usually win more often.

Why?
Because anyone with even a basic understanding of mahjong chooses their discards against riichi.

When you riichi on a wait that is not ryanmen,
tiles often come out later because of suji and kabe reasoning.
Those become your ron tiles. See Chapter 7 for more on that.

In this hand, if you choose the shanpon,
the relevant "effective tiles after riichi" include:

- Suji from <img src="../../hai/pin5.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou4.gif" width="24" height="34">
- Kabe from <img src="../../hai/pin3.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/pin4.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou5.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou6.gif" width="24" height="34">

That gives six different relationships that can make your winning tiles come out.

If you turn it into a kanchan instead,
then the only <span class="b">effective tiles after riichi</span> are
<img src="../../hai/sou5.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou6.gif" width="24" height="34"><img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34">.

And if you riichi after cutting <img src="../../hai/sou7.gif" width="24" height="34">,
then whichever of those appears on the table,
opponents are likely to become more wary of <img src="../../hai/sou8.gif" width="24" height="34">.

For those reasons, when the choice is between shanpon and kanchan (or penchan),

<span class="b">shanpon riichi is the basic theory.</span>

Of course, there are still many situations in real games where the kanchan is better,
so you should stay flexible.
